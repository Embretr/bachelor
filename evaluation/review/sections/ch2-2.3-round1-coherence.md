## Critical issues

### C1 — §2.3 ¶2 sentence 2: literature-anchor language for Norwegian vendors
**Quote:** "The two commercial Norwegian TMS that recur in the literature on the domain are Timpex and Opter; both handle order management, dispatch records, and invoicing, and neither generates assignment plans automatically."

**Problem:** The phrase "recur in the literature on the domain" asserts a literature-anchored claim, but no academic source in BIB_SLICE names Timpex or Opter. Norwegian TMS naming is project-context (interviews + supervisor briefing), not Ch 2 literature. Per the chapter-purity rule, Ch 2 must be source-anchored, not interview-grounded.

**Fix:** Drop the literature-framing language. Reframe as theory-of-the-domain factual description: "Two commercial Norwegian TMS named in the project's domain context are Timpex and Opter; both cover order management and invoicing, and neither generates assignment plans automatically." (Or: "Within the Norwegian market, Timpex and Opter are the two commercial TMS named in the project's domain context...").

### C2 — §2.3 ¶2 sentence 4: unsupported European generalisation
**Quote:** "a pattern compatible with what the literature describes for road freight in Europe more generally; running a TMS is not universal, and several transport companies operate without one"

**Problem:** Generalises Griffis's 2007 US 50% manual-management figure to "Europe more generally" without a European source. Heinbach et al. study platform providers, not adoption rates among end-user transport companies.

**Fix:** Cut "a pattern compatible with what the literature describes for road freight in Europe more generally;" entirely; keep "running a TMS is not universal, and several transport companies operate without one." This preserves the supervisor's required non-universal framing while removing the unsupported European claim.

## Minor issues

### M1 — §2.3 ¶1: heinbach attribution opacity
**Quote:** `\textcite{heinbach2022datadriven}` define a TMS as the category of software...

**Problem:** Heinbach et al. do not define TMS themselves; they cite the definition from De Muynck et al. (2020, Gartner). Source notes flag this explicitly.

**Fix:** Replace "define" with "adopt" or "cite": `\textcite{heinbach2022datadriven}` adopt a definition of TMS as ...

### M2 — §2.3 ¶2: mixed-concept paragraph
Three concept layers: (a) Norwegian vendor naming, (b) European platform research, (c) adoption variability. Topic sentence needs "and" to cover the paragraph. Restructure or close with a single hedge.

### M3 — §2.3 ¶1/¶2/¶3: operational-scope vocabulary drift
- ¶1: "order management, route handling, carrier coordination, and freight billing"
- ¶2: "order management, dispatch records, and invoicing"
- ¶3: "order intake, billing, and shipment-level execution"

"Freight billing" / "invoicing" / "billing" name the same activity; "order management" / "order intake" name the same activity; "route handling" disappears after ¶1. Lock ¶1's four-item list as canonical and reuse the same nouns in ¶2 and ¶3.
