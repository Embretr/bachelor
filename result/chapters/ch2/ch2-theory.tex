\section{Resource Scheduling}
\label{sec:resource-scheduling}

Resource scheduling concerns the assignment of limited resources to tasks over time, subject to constraints on availability, task requirements, and temporal dependencies \parencite{pinedo2016scheduling}. \textcite{pinedo2016scheduling} defines the general scheduling problem as determining when and how to process a set of jobs using a set of machines, where each job has processing requirements and each machine has capacity limits. In transport operations, the ``machines'' are drivers and vehicles, and the ``jobs'' are assignments with fixed time windows. This structure appears across several related domains. Nurse scheduling matches staff to shifts based on qualifications and availability. Crew scheduling in airlines assigns pilots and cabin crew to flight legs under regulatory rest requirements. Driver scheduling in public transit pairs operators with runs across a service day. All of these problems share the same core structure: matching a pool of constrained resources to a set of time-bound tasks.

Ressursplanlegger adds a layer of complexity by assigning \emph{both} an employee and a vehicle to each assignment. This makes it a multi-resource scheduling problem. Where a single-resource problem (e.g., assigning only drivers) must search through $n \times m$ possible pairings of $n$ drivers and $m$ assignments, a two-resource problem must also select a vehicle for each pairing. The number of feasible combinations grows quickly. In Ressursplanlegger, each assignment is a task with a fixed time window and a set of resource requirements: a driver with the right competencies and availability, and a vehicle of the correct type that is not already in use. The objective is to maximise assignment coverage while balancing several secondary goals, such as even workload distribution and respect for driver preferences.

The constraints in this problem fall into two categories. Hard constraints must be satisfied for a plan to be feasible. If any hard constraint is violated, the plan is invalid regardless of how well it performs on other measures. In Ressursplanlegger, the hard constraints are: an employee must hold all competencies required by the assignment (HC-01); the employee must be available and not already assigned (HC-02); the vehicle must be in active status (HC-03); the vehicle type must match the assignment's requirement (HC-04); and neither the employee (HC-05) nor the vehicle (HC-06) may be double-booked.

Soft constraints, by contrast, represent preferences that the algorithm tries to satisfy but may violate at a cost. Each soft constraint carries a configurable weight that determines how much its violation affects the total solution score. The soft constraints include preferring an employee's designated vehicle (weight 10), balancing workload evenly (weight 20), minimising transitions between consecutive assignments (weight 5), scheduling high-priority assignments first (weight 15), and respecting stated employee preferences (weight 10). This distinction between hard and soft constraints follows the standard constraint satisfaction framework described by \textcite{rossi2006handbook}, where a feasible solution satisfies all hard constraints and an optimal solution additionally minimises the weighted sum of soft constraint violations.

Resource scheduling problems of this kind are NP-hard (meaning no known algorithm can guarantee an optimal solution in polynomial time) at realistic fleet sizes \parencite{pinedo2016scheduling}. An exact solver that guarantees an optimal solution must, in the worst case, explore an exponential number of combinations. For small instances this is tractable, but as the number of assignments, drivers, and vehicles grows, computation time becomes impractical. This motivates the use of heuristic and metaheuristic methods that trade optimality guarantees for speed. Ressursplanlegger addresses this with a multi-engine approach: three solvers, each suited to different problem sizes. A related theoretical area is the Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP), first formalised by \textcite{dantzig1959truck} and surveyed by \textcite{toth2014vrp}. VRP and its time-windowed variant (VRPTW) share structural similarities with driver-vehicle assignment: both involve assigning resources to geographically distributed tasks under time and capacity constraints. However, the problems diverge in one important respect. VRP is primarily a sequencing problem: the algorithm decides the order in which a vehicle visits locations, and routing cost depends on travel distances between stops. In Ressursplanlegger, assignment times and locations are fixed by the customer. The algorithm decides \emph{who} performs each assignment, not \emph{in which order}. Sequencing is not part of the problem.

The three solvers in Ressursplanlegger occupy different points on the speed-quality tradeoff. The greedy solver sorts assignments by priority and applies a first-fit heuristic, assigning each task to the first driver-vehicle pair that satisfies all hard constraints. It runs in $O(n \times m)$ time, where $n$ is the number of assignments and $m$ is the number of available resources, and produces a result almost instantly. It offers no optimality guarantee. The CP-SAT solver, based on Google OR-Tools, is a complete constraint programming solver that models the full set of hard and soft constraints and searches for the best feasible solution within a configurable time limit (default 60 seconds). It produces near-optimal results for instances of up to roughly 500 assignments. For larger instances (1\,000+ assignments), the system uses Timefold, a Java-based metaheuristic engine that combines tabu search, simulated annealing, and late-acceptance strategies to explore the solution space. Timefold does not guarantee optimality but scales better than CP-SAT on large problems. The theoretical basis for constraint programming as a solving paradigm is described by \textcite{rossi2006handbook}, who characterise it as a method for modelling and solving problems defined by variables, domains, and constraints. This multi-solver architecture allows the traffic coordinator to choose the appropriate solver for the problem at hand, trading computation time for solution quality depending on the day's complexity. The choice of solver, however, is only one part of producing a usable plan; the following section examines the role of human oversight in validating and adjusting algorithm-generated schedules.


\section{Human-in-the-Loop Automation}
\label{sec:hitl}

\textcite{parasuraman2000automation} propose a taxonomy of automation that distinguishes both the \emph{type} of function being automated (information acquisition, information analysis, decision selection, and action implementation) and the \emph{level} of human involvement, ranging from fully manual control at level 1 to fully autonomous execution at level 10. At intermediate levels, the automated system generates options or makes a recommendation, but a human retains authority to review, modify, or reject the output before it takes effect. Ressursplanlegger occupies levels 5 to 6 on this scale: the algorithm generates a complete assignment plan and presents it to the traffic coordinator, who then reviews each assignment, applies corrections, and explicitly approves the plan before it becomes operative. Automation handles the combinatorial search; the human handles the final judgement.

This split is not merely a design preference; it reflects the nature of transport operations. Planning in this domain is subject to a class of disruptions that cannot be fully encoded in any constraint model: last-minute cancellations, road conditions, informal customer relationships, and the kind of tacit knowledge about individual drivers that coordinators accumulate over years of work. Which driver handles a particular customer well, which route demands local road familiarity, which employee is approaching the informal tolerance boundary for overtime that the company applies in practice. None of this can be read from a database. The interviews conducted for this study indicate that even coordinators who are broadly positive about automation expect to remain actively in the loop: the consensus is that the system should propose a plan the coordinator can correct, not replace the coordinator entirely. Full automation, in this context, is neither technically feasible nor organisationally acceptable.

The architectural consequence of this reasoning is what the system documentation describes as the \emph{suggest and override} pattern. The algorithm runs on demand and returns a scored, constraint-validated plan. The planning interface then displays this plan on a Gantt-style timeline, with each assignment showing its assigned driver and vehicle. The coordinator can drag assignments between rows to reassign them, remove assignments from the plan, or add manually constructed assignments that override the algorithm's decisions. Every such change is applied immediately and the affected assignments are re-scored. The interaction model positions the algorithm as a first-pass proposal and the coordinator as the authority who converts that proposal into a committed plan. The design is documented in the system's optimisation router, where a solution returned by any of the three solver engines is stored and made available to the frontend without triggering automatic assignment. Converting a solution into an operative plan requires an explicit coordinator action.

Whether coordinators will actually rely on a system of this kind depends in part on whether they trust its outputs. \textcite{lee2004trust} argue that trust in automation is calibrated through experience: operators develop beliefs about a system's performance and reliability, and adjust their reliance accordingly. The target condition is \emph{appropriate reliance} --- operators should engage with automation where it performs reliably and override it where it does not, rather than either rejecting it wholesale or deferring to it uncritically. Achieving this requires that the coordinator can inspect the system's reasoning, understand why a given assignment was made, and identify when the algorithm has produced something that contradicts their own knowledge. A system that returns a plan without explanation gives the coordinator nothing to work with when the output looks wrong. Trust, in this sense, is an interface problem as much as an algorithm problem.

This constraint shapes the design of the planning interface as directly as it shapes the algorithm itself. Ressursplanlegger addresses the transparency requirement through two mechanisms. The deviation viewer surfaces scheduling conflicts automatically: overbooking, overtime violations, missing competencies, expired certifications, and rest-period breaches are flagged as deviations and displayed as warnings the coordinator can act on. The solution scoring breakdown exposes the weighted contribution of each soft constraint to the total solution score, allowing the coordinator to see not just that one plan is better than another, but why. Together, these features are intended to give the coordinator enough information to make an informed correction rather than a blind one. The theoretical implication is that human-in-the-loop design is not complete once the override capability exists; the system must also expose enough of its internal state to make that override meaningful.


\section{Transport Management Systems}
\label{sec:tms}

The seven companies in this study all use some form of operations software to register transport orders and generate invoices; these products are generally called Transport Management Systems (TMS). In practice, TMS platforms handle what happens before and after an assignment is executed: order intake, status tracking, driver notification, and freight billing.

In the Norwegian small and medium-sized transport sector, three commercial TMS products were in use across the seven companies interviewed for this study: Timpex, Trimtex, and Opptur. Timpex is used by three of the Norlog subsidiaries; Trimtex is used by Norlog Tana; Opptur is used by Harlem Solutions. The remaining two companies operate their own custom order systems. In each case, the primary function these systems serve is order management and invoicing. Timpex and Opptur both provide driver-notification apps (Timpex Confirm and the Opptur order app respectively), which dispatch assignment information to drivers after the coordinator has made the decision manually. None of the systems provide algorithm-generated plans or automated driver-vehicle matching. All assignment decisions are made by the traffic coordinator and entered manually into the system. All three commercial systems handle order registration and billing, but provide no unified daily planning view, no conflict detection, no competency tracking with expiry management, and no capacity overview of available drivers and vehicles.

The gap these systems leave is specific and consistent across all seven companies: no existing system addresses the step between an order arriving in the system and a driver being assigned to carry it out. Order management and invoicing happen downstream of this decision, but the decision itself has no system support. Coordinators make it manually, from memory, using tacit knowledge about driver availability, competencies, and workload that is not captured in any system. The fit/gap analysis characterises this as the core gap: all existing systems focus on what happens \emph{after} the assignment is made, while the assignment decision itself remains entirely unmediated. Ressursplanlegger is designed to occupy precisely this space, providing algorithm-assisted planning support for the assignment step that existing TMS products leave unaddressed. The full feature-coverage comparison is in \Cref{sec:fitgap}.


\section{Design Science Research}
\label{sec:dsr-theory}

Design Science Research (DSR) is a research paradigm concerned with creating and evaluating artefacts that address practical problems in a given domain. \textcite{hevner2004dsr} establish DSR as a problem-solving paradigm distinct from behavioural science: where behavioural science seeks to understand natural phenomena, design science creates artefacts and evaluates their utility. The artefact itself (whether a software system, a model, a method, or a construct) is the primary research output. \textcite{peffers2007dsr} propose a six-phase process model for DSR in information systems: problem identification and motivation, objective definition, design and development, demonstration, evaluation, and communication. The value of this model is that it gives a structured account of how knowledge is produced through building: each phase both depends on and contributes to the others, and the evaluation of the artefact feeds back into understanding of the problem.

This thesis follows the DSR paradigm because its primary contribution is an artefact: Ressursplanlegger, a web-based resource planning platform for traffic coordinators in Norwegian transport companies. Building the system is not incidental to the research; it is the research. The problem is identified through user interviews, the requirements derived from those interviews shape the design, and the artefact is then assessed against those requirements. This cycle of problem-driven building and knowledge-producing evaluation is what DSR as a methodology describes. A purely empirical approach would study existing planning behaviour without producing a solution; a purely engineering approach would build the system without systematically investigating whether it solves the right problem. DSR combines both, and that combination is what the research question demands. The contribution of this work is therefore twofold: the artefact itself, and the knowledge gained about what such a system must do to be useful in this context.

The implications of \textcite{wieringa2014dsr}'s distinction between validation and evaluation --- and what that distinction means for what this thesis can and cannot claim --- are addressed in Chapter~3, which describes the specific application of DSR in this project.
