\chapter{Theory}
\label{chap:theory}

Ressursplanlegger (hereafter RP) pairs an algorithm that proposes daily plans with a coordinator who decides which of those plans actually take effect. Explaining that arrangement, and the choices it embodies, requires theory from four bodies of literature. Scheduling research supplies the vocabulary for the problem the algorithm solves and the utilization measures by which a finished plan is judged. Human factors research explains why a coordinator should retain authority over what the algorithm proposes, and how trust in such a system forms. Transport management research locates the planning gap that RP enters, against the systems already on the market. Design Science Research is the paradigm under which an artefact and the knowledge it embodies are constructed and validated together.

\section{Resource Scheduling}
\label{sec:scheduling}

Each morning a Norwegian traffic coordinator matches drivers and vehicles to the day's transport assignments. The scheduling literature treats this kind of task abstractly. \textcite{pinedo2016scheduling} defines scheduling as the allocation of resources to tasks over time, subject to given constraints, with the goal of optimising one or more objectives. The same problem family covers nurse rostering, airline crew scheduling, and driver scheduling, which \textcite{ernst2004staff} group as personnel scheduling, the construction of work timetables so that an organisation can satisfy demand for its services. In its simplest form scheduling assigns a single resource per task, as a charge nurse does when matching one nurse to each bed at the start of a hospital shift.

The RP problem extends this single-resource case in one critical way: every assignment requires both a driver and a vehicle, paired for the duration of the work. Translated into scheduling vocabulary, assignments are tasks with fixed time windows, fixed pickup and delivery locations, and resource requirements; drivers and vehicles are resources with competency, availability, and capacity attributes; the plan covers a single operational day. The pairing requirement raises combinatorial complexity beyond the single-resource models that dominate \parencite{pinedo2016scheduling}, because feasibility now depends on the compatibility of two resource types simultaneously.

Constraint satisfaction alone does not capture what makes a plan operationally good. Personnel-scheduling literature judges plans not only by feasibility but by utilization measures: overtime, idle time between consecutive assignments, and the evenness of workload across staff \parencite{ernst2004staff}. The thesis frames these dimensions under \textbf{Efficiency}, the anchor concept for improved resource utilization. Visibility is the precondition: utilization patterns that remain implicit in spreadsheets and whiteboards cannot be optimised, so the artefact's first contribution is to make them legible before any algorithm reorders them.

Constraints in this domain divide into two categories. \emph{Hard} constraints must hold for a plan to be feasible at all: the driver's licence class must match the vehicle, the driver must hold any required certifications (for example, an ADR certificate, named after the European agreement on road transport of dangerous goods, for hazardous cargo, or a crane operator certificate for cargo loaded by a truck-mounted crane), the driver must be on duty rather than on declared time-off, the vehicle type must match what the assignment requires, and no driver or vehicle may be booked on two assignments at the same time. \emph{Soft} constraints encode preferences that the plan should honour where possible but may be traded off, each weighted in the objective the solver minimises: even workload across drivers, individual driver preferences, continuity with the same customer over time, and smooth transitions between one assignment and the next. \textcite{ernst2004staff} state the modelling convention directly: hard constraints must be satisfied while soft constraints may be violated, the latter entering the objective as penalty terms. \textcite{rossi2006constraint} give the same logic in constraint-programming vocabulary, distinguishing deadlines that bound feasibility from due dates that enter the weighted objective. The weights on soft constraints are configurable per company in RP, so a coordinator's notion of a good plan can be expressed without code changes.

Such combinatorial assignment problems are NP-hard, meaning that the time required to find a guaranteed-optimal solution grows exponentially with the size of the input, so exhaustively searching every possible plan does not scale to the daily fleets of dozens of drivers and vehicles that the interviewed companies operate \parencite{rossi2006constraint, pinedo2016scheduling}. This is what motivates the multi-engine solver architecture revisited in \Cref{sec:iter-multiengine}: rather than seeking a provably optimal plan, the system uses solvers that return good plans within a time budget the coordinator sets. The literature on Vehicle Routing Problems (VRPs) addresses an adjacent but distinct question: a VRP minimises total route cost over a sequence of customer visits delivered by a fleet from a depot \parencite{braekers2016vrp}, treating the order of stops as the main decision variable. RP handles assignments with fixed locations and fixed time windows, so the open question is \emph{who} performs each assignment, not the order in which stops are visited. \textcite{braekers2016vrp} explicitly exclude problems that combine routing with scheduling decisions from their review, which is the boundary the present work sits on the scheduling side of.

Three families of solvers are commonly applied to such problems: constructive heuristics, constraint programming, and metaheuristics. Each is instantiated as a concrete engine in \Cref{sec:iter-multiengine}. \emph{Constructive heuristics}, which \textcite{pinedo2016scheduling} calls dispatching rules, build a plan one assignment at a time according to a priority rule (for example, "assign the most constrained job first"); they are fast but can settle on a locally good plan that is far from the best plan reachable by reordering. \emph{Constraint programming} formalises the problem as a set of variables and constraints and then searches the space of feasible plans systematically, using propagation (deducing forced choices from the constraints) and backtracking (undoing a choice when it leads to a dead end), returning near-optimal plans within a time budget at the cost of higher modelling effort \parencite{rossi2006constraint}. \emph{Metaheuristics} are general-purpose search strategies that explore the space of plans by repeatedly modifying a current candidate; the tabu search introduced by \textcite{glover1986future} keeps a short-term memory of recent moves to avoid cycling back to them, and the late-acceptance hill climbing of \textcite{burke2017late} accepts a new candidate if it is no worse than one seen a fixed number of steps ago, both of which let the search escape local optima on large instances. \textcite{ernst2004staff} report empirically that heuristics suit messy real-world rostering while constraint programming dominates on highly constrained formulations; each family occupies a different point on the speed-quality tradeoff that \Cref{sec:iter-multiengine} resolves for this artefact. Solver capability alone, however, says nothing about how the resulting plan reaches the road: who reads it, who can override it, and on what authority. That question is the subject of §\ref{sec:hitl}.

\section{Human-in-the-Loop Automation}
\label{sec:hitl}

A traffic coordinator who reviews and approves every algorithm-generated plan is operating in what the human factors literature calls a human-in-the-loop configuration: an automated process generates a recommendation, and a human exercises authority over whether it takes effect. \textcite{parasuraman2000automation} formalise this on a ten-level scale of automation for decision and action selection. The scale runs from level 1 (fully manual) to level 10 (fully autonomous), with intermediate steps that progressively transfer authority from human to machine. Level 4 is "the computer suggests one alternative", level 5 is "executes that suggestion if the human approves", and level 6 is "executes automatically unless the human vetoes within a time window". RP sits at level 5: the solver produces one plan, the plan is shown on the timeline, and no driver is dispatched until the coordinator approves it. The artefact deliberately stops short of level 6: the coordinator must act, not merely fail to object.

The choice of level 5 is not arbitrary, and the framing for why partial automation is the appropriate target comes from \textcite{bainbridge1983ironies}. Bainbridge's central observation is that the people who specify automation are not the same as the people who must operate it. The same split appears here: the call for algorithm-assisted planning comes from owners and the business side, while the traffic coordinator is the person who must run any such system day to day. Bainbridge argues that automation built under this asymmetry tends to leave operators with an "arbitrary collection of tasks" and inadequate support for them \parencite[p.~775]{bainbridge1983ironies}. Grounding HITL on this asymmetry rather than on convenience makes \textbf{Trust/control} a structural requirement of the system, not a usability feature: the coordinator must be able to inspect, modify, accept, or reject any algorithm-generated assignment, because the system was specified by people who do not bear the consequences of its mistakes. Bainbridge's argument is theoretical here; the empirical extension to Norwegian transport belongs in Chapter 4.

The structural argument for coordinator authority does not yet say how trust in the algorithm forms in practice. \textcite{hoff2015trust} review 127 studies in human-automation trust from 101 articles, and synthesise them into a three-layer model of dispositional, situational, and learned trust \parencite[p.~413]{hoff2015trust}. Dispositional trust reflects an individual's stable tendency to trust automation. Situational trust varies with task context and workload. Learned trust is built through experience with the specific system: first as initial trust based on reputation, then as dynamic trust that updates with each interaction \parencite[p.~420]{hoff2015trust}. The model implies that two coordinators using the same plan generator can hold different levels of trust at the same moment, and that the same coordinator's reliance on the algorithm shifts across days. Hoff and Bashir also report that early errors damage trust more than later errors do \parencite[p.~426]{hoff2015trust}, which is a design constraint at deployment time: a coordinator's first week with RP weighs more in trust formation than the average of the months that follow. The model's findings are averaged across populations and drawn primarily from aviation and military settings, so transfer to a small Norwegian coordinator pool is by analogy rather than direct empirical support. By analogy, the model gives the project not a quantitative prediction but a design constraint: calibrated trust (neither over- nor under-reliance) is the target the artefact must enable through the override and explanation interfaces that the next two layers specify.

Calibration depends on the coordinator being able to interpret what the algorithm has done. \textcite{miller2019explanation} surveys explanation in the social sciences and argues that explanation is contrastive, selective, and social: people ask "Why P rather than Q?", they expect one or two relevant causes rather than a complete causal chain, and they treat explanation as a communicative act tailored to what they already know \parencite[p.~3]{miller2019explanation}. For an algorithm-assisted plan, the question is rarely "Why was this assignment generated?" It is "Why was this driver assigned rather than the one I would have picked?" Miller frames explanation as an interface property of automated decision systems and argues that "trust is lost when users cannot understand traces of observed behaviour or decisions" \parencite[p.~3]{miller2019explanation}. The implication for RP is that the system must show contrastive reasons for its choices in the coordinator's vocabulary, such as competence, time, and current load, rather than expose raw constraint weights and solver internals. Explanation becomes a first-class part of the HITL interface, alongside the override controls themselves.

A fourth layer sits beneath these three. \textcite{lee2004trust} define trust as "the attitude that an agent will help achieve an individual's goals in a situation characterized by uncertainty and vulnerability" \parencite[p.~54]{lee2004trust}, and argue that the design goal is appropriate trust, not greater trust. A coordinator who accepts every plan without review (over-reliance, what Lee and See call \textit{misuse}) and a coordinator who reverts to fully manual planning despite a competent solver (under-reliance, \textit{disuse}) are both failures of trust calibration. Lee and See's design principles for trustable automation specify that the system should reveal its past performance, expose its process, and communicate its purpose in terms that connect to the user's goals \parencite[p.~74]{lee2004trust}. In the artefact this thesis evaluates, those three dimensions correspond to deviation history, scoring breakdown, and the explicit framing of the system as decision support rather than decision maker. The threat that Lee and See identify is concrete: if coordinators do not understand or trust the algorithm, they will not adopt it, and the visibility gap §2.1 framed under \textbf{Efficiency} will remain unaddressed regardless of solver quality. Chapter 5 returns to this adoption question.

These layers translate into concrete design constraints on the artefact's HITL interface. The planning timeline at \texttt{/ressursplanlegger} shows the algorithm's suggested plan in the same Gantt view the coordinator uses for manual work, so the suggestion is open to inspection rather than presented as a finished output. After a solver run, a summary panel reports the solution score and counts of hard and soft violations, which exposes process and performance in the sense Lee and See describe. The deviation viewer at \texttt{/avvik} lists each active conflict by type, affected assignment, and affected resource, so a coordinator who disagrees with the algorithm can locate the contested assignment and decide whether to override. Override is intentionally low-friction: dragging an assignment block to a different employee row reassigns it, and conflicting changes save with a warning rather than a block. That choice is deliberate. A blocking validator would tell the coordinator that the system, not the coordinator, holds final authority, which is precisely the configuration Bainbridge warns against and the one Trust/control forbids. Soft constraints are weighted per company so that a coordinator's notion of a good plan can be adjusted without code changes, and the choice of solver engine and time budget is exposed at the point of plan generation so that capability limits are legible before the plan is read. These mechanisms are not features added to a finished automation; they are what makes the automation operable at all. \Cref{sec:iter-hitl,sec:iter-deviations,sec:iter-tradeoff} document how each layer was built into the artefact.

\section{Transport Management Systems}
\label{sec:tms}

The traffic coordinator who builds the daily plan does so in software called a Transport Management System (TMS). \textcite{heinbach2022datadriven} define TMS as software that plans and executes the physical movement of goods across supply chains \parencite[p.~811]{heinbach2022datadriven}, a definition the same authors trace back to TMS as freight-forwarder software for assigning customer shipments to fleet resources. \textcite{griffis2007tms} give a compatible definition from a North American shipper survey: TMS is information technology used to plan, optimise, and execute transportation operations before, during, and after the physical movement of goods \parencite[p.~19]{griffis2007tms}. Across the rest of this section, "TMS" covers order management, route handling, carrier coordination, and freight billing.

Within that scope, current commercial systems vary in coverage. In the Norwegian market, the two commercial TMS named in the seven interviews are \textbf{Timpex} and \textbf{Opter}; both cover order management and freight billing, and neither generates assignment plans automatically. Beyond order and invoicing tools, \textcite{heinbach2022datadriven} catalogue eleven European platform providers and identify TRUCKAHEAD as a transport workflow platform that pairs dispatcher–driver communication with workflow automation \parencite[p.~819]{heinbach2022datadriven}. Adoption itself varies. \textcite{griffis2007tms} report that among the firms in their 2007 North American sample without any TMS, half manage transportation entirely manually, while the other half rely on partial or legacy IT \parencite[pp.~26]{griffis2007tms}, so a substantial share of transport operations run without dedicated planning software at all.

These systems cover part of the TMS scope but leave a gap inside it. \textcite{griffis2007tms} observe that most TMS offerings centre on the individual shipment as the unit of analysis and lack the ability to optimise multi-load shipments, leaving load consolidation as a manual activity \parencite[p.~27]{griffis2007tms}. \textcite{heinbach2022datadriven} reach the same conclusion: TMS products focus on shipment-level business activities, and other digital platforms add capabilities outside that focus \parencite[p.~808]{heinbach2022datadriven}. What existing systems cover, in short, is order management, freight billing, and shipment-level execution. What is missing is the upstream planning step between an order existing and a specific driver and vehicle being assigned to perform it. Locating this missing step against the named systems is theoretical here; \Cref{chap:findings} substantiates it with the empirical comparison. The variation in vendor coverage and sector adoption is itself part of what the artefact must accommodate; the per-company configurability that delivers it is built in \Cref{sec:iter-weights}, and the discussion returns to it under \textbf{Adaptability} in §5.1.3. §\ref{sec:dsr} turns to the research paradigm under which an artefact filling that gap is built and investigated.

\section{Design Science Research}
\label{sec:dsr}

Design Science Research is the research paradigm in which a designed artefact is constructed and investigated as the central contribution of a study, together with the knowledge produced about how that artefact behaves in its context. \textcite{wieringa2014dsm} states the position concisely: design science is the design and investigation of artefacts in context, where the artefact is built to improve something in its problem context \parencite[p.~3]{wieringa2014dsm}. \textcite{hevner2007threecycle} structures the same work as three linked cycles. The relevance cycle imports requirements and acceptance criteria from the application environment; the rigor cycle draws on the existing scientific knowledge base and contributes back to it; the design cycle iterates internally between constructing and evaluating the artefact \parencite[pp.~88--90]{hevner2007threecycle}. \textcite{peffers2007dsrm} structure the same paradigm as a procedural model. Their Design Science Research Methodology (DSRM) specifies six activities: problem identification and motivation; objectives for a solution; design and development; demonstration; evaluation; and communication \parencite[p.~46]{peffers2007dsrm}. The two framings are complementary: the cycles describe what must be present, the activities describe what is done. Chapter 3 applies the DSRM activities step by step to this project.

DSR is not the only paradigm available for an information systems study, and two alternatives in the IS literature warrant comparison. \textcite{orlikowski1991studying} survey the IS literature and organise it into three philosophical stances: positivist, interpretive, and critical \parencite[p.~5]{orlikowski1991studying}. The positivist stance assumes an objective social reality investigated with structured instrumentation, with the goal of testing hypotheses to increase predictive understanding \parencite[p.~5]{orlikowski1991studying}; this fits a study that measures population-level effects of a deployed system, but on its own it cannot justify building a new artefact whose primary contribution is the design knowledge embedded in it. The interpretive stance assumes that organisational reality is socially constructed and seeks contextual understanding through participants' meanings rather than generalisable laws \parencite[p.~5]{orlikowski1991studying}; this fits a study whose contribution is rich description of coordinator practice, but it cannot, on its own, support building a runnable artefact whose behaviour can be checked against external criteria. DSR is the third paradigm the present project requires: an artefact-plus-knowledge contribution in which qualitative input informs requirements and the artefact itself is examined against those requirements.

A second distinction inside DSR shapes what kind of empirical evidence is acceptable. \textcite{wieringa2014dsm} distinguishes validation from evaluation: validation predicts whether a deployed system would do what stakeholders want, while evaluation observes a deployed system in actual use \parencite[p.~31]{wieringa2014dsm}. In validation the researcher experiments with a model of how stakeholders would use the artefact; in evaluation stakeholders use the artefact independently of the researcher \parencite[p.~34]{wieringa2014dsm}. The present thesis validates rather than evaluates RP. Behaviour is predicted through benchmarking on synthetic datasets, which is a single-case mechanism experiment in Wieringa's classification (one artefact, controlled inputs) \parencite[p.~64]{wieringa2014dsm}, and through requirements traceability against the requirements derived in the relevance cycle; no transport company has operated the system independently. \textcite{hevner2007threecycle} treats this exact ordering as expected practice, with artefacts tested in laboratory and experimental situations before being released into field testing along the relevance cycle \parencite[p.~91]{hevner2007threecycle}, so the absence of production deployment is a structural feature of the project's stage rather than a methodological deficit. \Cref{sec:evaluation-framework} applies this distinction to the synthetic-benchmark protocol, and Section 5.4 returns to it as limitation L5 when bounding what that validation can and cannot claim.

The remainder of the methodological argument belongs to Chapter 3. \Cref{chap:method} applies the six DSRM activities step by step to this specific project, naming the inputs, the artefact outputs, and the validation instruments at each step. The validation versus evaluation distinction reappears in §3.6 to bound what the empirical work can and cannot claim.
