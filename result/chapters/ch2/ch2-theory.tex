\chapter{Theory}
\label{chap:theory}

This chapter establishes four theoretical foundations on which the rest of the thesis stands. Resource scheduling under a utilization lens (§\ref{sec:scheduling}) supplies the problem vocabulary and preloads \textbf{Effektivitet}; a five-layer human-in-the-loop theory (§\ref{sec:hitl}) supplies the configuration vocabulary and preloads \textbf{Tillit/kontroll}; Transport Management Systems (§\ref{sec:tms}) position the category Ressursplanlegger sits inside and preload \textbf{Tilpasningsdyktighet}; and Design Science Research (§\ref{sec:dsr}) supplies the research paradigm under which an artefact-plus-knowledge contribution is built and investigated. Section length is asymmetric on purpose: §\ref{sec:hitl} carries the most argumentative load and is the longest, while Vehicle Routing theory inside §\ref{sec:scheduling} and TMS-as-category in §\ref{sec:tms} are referenced narrowly --- the former to delimit, the latter to locate the planning gap the artefact fills.

\section{Resource Scheduling}
\label{sec:scheduling}

Each morning a Norwegian traffic coordinator matches drivers and vehicles to the day's transport assignments. The scheduling literature treats this kind of task abstractly. \textcite{pinedo2016scheduling} defines scheduling as the allocation of resources to tasks over time, subject to given constraints, with the goal of optimising one or more objectives. The same problem family covers nurse rostering, airline crew scheduling, and driver scheduling, which \textcite{ernst2004staff} group as personnel scheduling, the construction of work timetables so that an organisation can satisfy demand for its services. In its simplest form scheduling assigns a single resource per task, as a charge nurse does when matching one nurse to each bed at the start of a hospital shift.

The Ressursplanlegger problem extends this single-resource case along one decisive axis: every assignment requires both a driver and a vehicle, paired for the duration of the work. Translated into scheduling vocabulary, assignments are tasks with fixed time windows, fixed pickup and delivery locations, and resource requirements; drivers and vehicles are resources with competency, availability, and capacity attributes; the plan covers a single operational day. The pairing requirement raises combinatorial complexity beyond the single-resource models that dominate \parencite{pinedo2016scheduling}, because feasibility now depends on the compatibility of two resource types simultaneously.

Constraint satisfaction alone does not capture what makes a plan operationally good. Personnel-scheduling literature judges plans not only by feasibility but by utilization measures: overtime, idle time between consecutive assignments, and the evenness of workload across staff \parencite{ernst2004staff}. The thesis frames these dimensions under \textbf{Effektivitet}, the anchor concept for improved resource utilization. Visibility is the precondition: utilization patterns that remain implicit in spreadsheets and whiteboards cannot be optimised, so the artefact's first contribution is to make them legible before any algorithm reorders them.

Constraints in this domain divide into two categories. Hard constraints must hold for a plan to be feasible: driver licence class, ADR or crane competence, declared availability and time-off, vehicle type compatibility, and the prohibition on double-booking a driver or a vehicle. Soft constraints encode preferences with configurable weights, including workload balance, driver preferences, customer continuity, and smooth transitions between consecutive assignments. \textcite{ernst2004staff} state the modelling convention directly: hard constraints must be satisfied while soft constraints may be violated and are represented as penalty terms in the objective. \textcite{rossi2006constraint} give the same logic in constraint-programming vocabulary, distinguishing deadlines that bound feasibility from due dates that enter the weighted objective. Soft-constraint weights in Ressursplanlegger are coordinator-configurable per company.

Such combinatorial assignment problems are NP-hard, so exact enumeration does not scale to realistic instance sizes: a guaranteed-optimal solver becomes intractable on the daily fleets of dozens of drivers and vehicles that interviewed companies operate, which is what motivates the multi-engine architecture revisited in \Cref{sec:algorithm-design} \parencite{rossi2006constraint, pinedo2016scheduling}. The literature on Vehicle Routing Problems addresses an adjacent but distinct question: VRPs minimise route cost over sequenced customer visits delivered by a fleet from a depot \parencite{braekers2016vrp}. Ressursplanlegger handles fixed-location, fixed-window assignments, so the open question is who performs each assignment, not in what order stops are visited. \textcite{braekers2016vrp} explicitly exclude problems that combine routing with scheduling decisions from their review, which clarifies the boundary the present work sits on the scheduling side of.

Three solver families are commonly applied to such problems: constructive heuristics, constraint programming, and metaheuristics. Each is revisited in \Cref{sec:algorithm-design}. Constructive heuristics, which \textcite{pinedo2016scheduling} calls dispatching rules, build a plan one assignment at a time according to a priority rule; they are fast but may stall in local optima. Constraint programming searches the feasible space with propagation and backtracking and returns near-optimal plans within a time budget at the cost of higher modelling effort \parencite{rossi2006constraint}. Metaheuristics, including the tabu search introduced by \textcite{glover1986future} and the late-acceptance hill climbing of \textcite{burke2017late}, escape local optima through structured exploration on large instances. \textcite{ernst2004staff} report empirically that heuristics suit messy real-world rostering while constraint programming dominates on highly constrained formulations; each family occupies a different point on the speed-quality tradeoff that \Cref{sec:algorithm-design} resolves for this artefact. Solver capability alone, however, says nothing about how the resulting plan reaches the road: who reads it, who can override it, and on what authority. That question is the subject of §\ref{sec:hitl}.

\section{Human-in-the-Loop Automation}
\label{sec:hitl}

A traffic coordinator who reviews and approves every algorithm-generated plan is operating in what the human factors literature calls a human-in-the-loop configuration: an automated process generates a recommendation, and a human exercises authority over whether it takes effect. \textcite{parasuraman2000automation} formalise this configuration on a ten-level scale of automation for decision and action selection, where level 1 is fully manual, level 10 is fully autonomous, level 4 is "the computer suggests one alternative", and level 5 is "executes that suggestion if the human approves". Ressursplanlegger sits at level 5: the solver layer produces one plan, the plan is shown on the timeline, and no driver is dispatched until the coordinator approves it. The artefact deliberately stops short of level 6, where the system would execute automatically unless the coordinator vetoed within a time window. The coordinator must act, not merely fail to object.

The choice of level 5 is not arbitrary, and the framing for why partial automation is the appropriate target comes from \textcite{bainbridge1983ironies}. Bainbridge's central observation is that demand for automation is articulated by those who design and own the system, while the residual operator role is left to whoever must run it day to day. The same configuration appears in the resource-planning context this thesis addresses: the call for algorithm-assisted planning comes from owners and the business side, while the traffic coordinator is the party who must operate any such system. Bainbridge argues that automation built under this asymmetry tends to leave operators with an "arbitrary collection of tasks" and inadequate support for them \parencite[p.~775]{bainbridge1983ironies}. Anchoring HITL on this asymmetry rather than on convenience makes \textbf{Tillit/kontroll} a structural requirement of the system, not a usability feature: the coordinator must be able to inspect, modify, accept, or reject any algorithm-generated assignment, because the system was specified by parties who do not bear the consequences of its mistakes. Bainbridge's argument is theoretical here; the empirical extension to Norwegian transport belongs in Chapter 4.

The structural argument for coordinator authority does not yet say how trust in the algorithm forms in practice. \textcite{hoff2015trust} review the empirical literature on human-automation trust across 127 studies drawn from 101 articles and synthesise them into a three-layer model of dispositional, situational, and learned trust \parencite[p.~413]{hoff2015trust}. Dispositional trust reflects an individual's stable tendency to trust automation, situational trust varies with task context and workload, and learned trust is built through experience with the specific system, first as initial trust based on reputation and then as dynamic trust that updates with each interaction \parencite[p.~420]{hoff2015trust}. The model implies that two coordinators using the same plan generator can hold different levels of trust at the same moment, and that the same coordinator's reliance on the algorithm shifts across days. Hoff and Bashir also report that early errors leave a disproportionately large negative imprint on learned trust \parencite[p.~426]{hoff2015trust}, which is a design constraint at deployment time: a coordinator's first week with Ressursplanlegger weighs more in trust formation than the average of the months that follow. The model's findings are aggregate, drawn primarily from aviation and military settings, so transfer to a small Norwegian coordinator pool is by analogy rather than direct empirical support. The implication this analogy supports is not a quantitative prediction but a design constraint: calibrated trust --- neither over- nor under-reliance --- is the target the artefact must enable through the operability and explanation surfaces that the next two layers specify.

Calibration depends on the coordinator being able to interpret what the algorithm has done. \textcite{miller2019explanation} surveys explanation in the social sciences and argues that explanation is contrastive, selective, and social: people ask "Why P rather than Q?", they expect one or two relevant causes rather than a complete causal chain, and they treat explanation as a communicative act tailored to what they already know \parencite[p.~3]{miller2019explanation}. For an algorithm-assisted plan, the question is rarely "Why was this assignment generated?" It is "Why was this driver assigned rather than the one I would have picked?" Miller frames explanation as an interface property of automated decision systems and argues that "trust is lost when users cannot understand traces of observed behaviour or decisions" \parencite[p.~3]{miller2019explanation}. The implication for Ressursplanlegger is that the system must surface contrastive reasons for its choices in the coordinator's vocabulary, such as competence, time, and current load, rather than expose raw constraint weights and solver internals. Explanation becomes a first-class part of the HITL surface, alongside the override controls themselves.

These three layers, the asymmetry frame, the trust model, and the explanation requirement, sit on top of an older foundation. \textcite{lee2004trust} define trust as "the attitude that an agent will help achieve an individual's goals in a situation characterized by uncertainty and vulnerability" \parencite[p.~54]{lee2004trust}, and they argue that the design goal is appropriate trust, not greater trust. A coordinator who accepts every plan without review (over-reliance, what Lee and See call \textit{misuse}) and a coordinator who reverts to fully manual planning despite a competent solver (under-reliance, \textit{disuse}) are both failures of trust calibration. Lee and See's design principles for trustable automation specify that the system should reveal its past performance, expose its process, and communicate its purpose in terms that connect to the user's goals \parencite[p.~74]{lee2004trust}. In the artefact this thesis evaluates, those three dimensions correspond to deviation history, scoring breakdown, and the explicit framing of the system as decision support rather than decision maker. The threat that Lee and See identify is concrete: if coordinators do not understand or trust the algorithm, they will not adopt it, and the visibility gap §2.1 framed under \textbf{Effektivitet} will remain unaddressed regardless of solver quality. Chapter 5 returns to this adoption question.

These layers translate into concrete design constraints on the artefact's HITL surface. The planning timeline at \texttt{/ressursplanlegger} shows the algorithm's suggested plan in the same Gantt view the coordinator uses for manual work, so the suggestion is open to inspection rather than presented as a finished output. After a solver run, a summary panel reports the solution score and counts of hard and soft violations, which exposes process and performance in the sense Lee and See describe. The deviation viewer at \texttt{/avvik} lists each active conflict by type, affected assignment, and affected resource, so a coordinator who disagrees with the algorithm can locate the contested assignment and decide whether to override. Override itself is engineered to be low-friction: dragging an assignment block to a different employee row reassigns it, and conflicting changes save with a warning rather than a block. That choice is deliberate. A blocking validator would tell the coordinator that the system, not the coordinator, holds final authority, which is precisely the configuration Bainbridge warns against and the one Tillit/kontroll forbids. Soft constraints are weighted per company so that a coordinator's notion of a good plan can be adjusted without code changes, and the choice of solver engine and time budget is exposed at the point of plan generation so that capability limits are legible before the plan is read. These mechanisms are not features added to a finished automation; they are what makes the automation operable at all.

\section{Transport Management Systems}
\label{sec:tms}

The traffic coordinator who builds the daily plan does so inside, or alongside, a piece of software the industry calls a Transport Management System. \textcite{heinbach2022datadriven} adopt a definition of TMS as the category of software that deals with the planning and execution of the physical movement of goods across supply chains \parencite[p.~811]{heinbach2022datadriven}, a definition the same authors trace back to TMS as freight-forwarder software for assigning customer shipments to fleet resources. \textcite{griffis2007tms} give a compatible reading from a North American shipper survey, characterising TMS as information technology used to plan, optimise, and execute transportation operations across the pre-, during-, and post-movement phases \parencite[p.~19]{griffis2007tms}. The operational scope established here, planning and execution across order management, route handling, carrier coordination, and freight billing, is the sense in which TMS is used through the rest of this section.

Within that scope, the live state-of-the-art is uneven across vendors and across the sector. Within the Norwegian market, the two commercial TMS named in this thesis's domain context are \textbf{Timpex} and \textbf{Opter}; both cover order management and freight billing, and neither generates assignment plans automatically. European platform research extends the picture beyond order and invoicing tools: \textcite{heinbach2022datadriven} catalogue eleven platform providers and identify TRUCKAHEAD as a transport workflow platform that pairs dispatcher–driver communication with workflow automation \parencite[p.~819]{heinbach2022datadriven}. Adoption itself is variable. \textcite{griffis2007tms} report that fifty per cent of non-users in their 2007 North American sample manage transportation manually rather than with a TMS \parencite[p.~26]{griffis2007tms}; running a TMS is not universal, and several transport companies operate without one.

The systems just named occupy a recognisable slice of the TMS scope, but they leave a gap inside it. \textcite{griffis2007tms} observe that most TMS offerings centre on the individual shipment as the unit of analysis and lack the ability to optimise multi-load shipments, leaving load consolidation as a manual activity \parencite[p.~27]{griffis2007tms}. \textcite{heinbach2022datadriven} reach a structurally similar conclusion: TMS products address core business activities at the shipment level, while supplementary digital platforms enrich the operational processes that fall outside that core \parencite[p.~808]{heinbach2022datadriven}. What existing systems cover, in short, is order management, freight billing, and shipment-level execution; what is missing is the upstream planning step between an order existing and a specific driver and vehicle being assigned to perform it. Locating this missing step against the named systems is theoretical here; \Cref{chap:findings} substantiates it with the empirical comparison. That variability across vendor coverage and across sector adoption is itself part of what the artefact must accommodate; the discussion returns to it under \textbf{Tilpasningsdyktighet} in §5.1.3. Locating the planning gap inside the TMS category is one matter; specifying the research paradigm under which an artefact filling that gap is built and investigated is another. §\ref{sec:dsr} addresses the second.

\section{Design Science Research}
\label{sec:dsr}

Design Science Research is the research paradigm in which a designed artefact is constructed and investigated as the central contribution of a study, alongside the knowledge produced about how that artefact behaves in its context. \textcite{wieringa2014dsm} states the position concisely: design science is the design and investigation of artefacts in context, where the artefact is built to interact with a problem context in order to improve something there \parencite[p.~3]{wieringa2014dsm}. \textcite{hevner2007threecycle} structures the same enterprise as three interdependent cycles. The relevance cycle imports requirements and acceptance criteria from the application environment; the rigor cycle grounds the work in the existing scientific knowledge base and returns new knowledge to it; the design cycle iterates internally between constructing and evaluating the artefact \parencite[pp.~88--90]{hevner2007threecycle}. \textcite{peffers2007dsrm} render the same paradigm as a procedural model. Their Design Science Research Methodology (DSRM) specifies six activities: problem identification and motivation; objectives for a solution; design and development; demonstration; evaluation; and communication \parencite[p.~46]{peffers2007dsrm}. The two framings are complementary; the cycles describe what must be present, the activities describe what is done. Chapter 3 applies the DSRM activities step by step to this project.

This paradigm choice is not the only option available for an information systems study, and two alternatives in the IS literature warrant explicit comparison. \textcite{orlikowski1991studying} survey the IS literature and organise it into three philosophical stances: positivist, interpretive, and critical \parencite[p.~5]{orlikowski1991studying}. The positivist stance assumes an objective social reality investigated with structured instrumentation, with the goal of testing hypotheses to increase predictive understanding \parencite[p.~5]{orlikowski1991studying}; this fits a study that measures population-level effects of a deployed system, but it does not on its own license the construction of a new artefact whose primary contribution is the design knowledge embedded in it. The interpretive stance assumes that organisational reality is socially constructed and seeks contextual understanding through participants' meanings rather than generalisable laws \parencite[p.~5]{orlikowski1991studying}; this fits a study whose contribution is rich description of coordinator practice, but it does not on its own license building a runnable artefact whose behaviour can be checked against external criteria. DSR is the synthesis the present project requires: an artefact-plus-knowledge contribution in which qualitative input informs requirements and the artefact itself is examined against those requirements.

A second distinction inside DSR shapes what counts as a defensible empirical anchor. \textcite{wieringa2014dsm} distinguishes validation from evaluation: validation justifies that a treatment would contribute to stakeholder goals if implemented, while evaluation investigates a treatment as applied by stakeholders in the field \parencite[p.~31]{wieringa2014dsm}. In validation the researcher experiments with a model of how stakeholders would use the artefact; in evaluation stakeholders use the artefact independently of the researcher \parencite[p.~34]{wieringa2014dsm}. The present thesis validates rather than evaluates Ressursplanlegger. Behaviour is predicted through benchmarking on synthetic datasets, which is a single-case mechanism experiment in Wieringa's classification \parencite[p.~64]{wieringa2014dsm}, and through requirements traceability against the requirements derived in the relevance cycle; no transport company has operated the system independently. \textcite{hevner2007threecycle} treats this exact ordering as expected practice --- artefacts are tested in laboratory and experimental situations before being released into field testing along the relevance cycle \parencite[p.~91]{hevner2007threecycle} --- so the absence of production deployment is a structural feature of the project's stage rather than a methodological deficit. Section 5.4 returns to this distinction in limitation L5 when bounding what the synthetic-benchmark validation can and cannot claim.

The remainder of the methodological argument belongs to Chapter 3. \Cref{chap:method} applies the six DSRM activities step by step to this specific project, naming the inputs, the artefact outputs, and the validation instruments at each step. The validation versus evaluation distinction reappears in §3.6 to bound what the empirical work can and cannot claim.
