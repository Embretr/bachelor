\section{Research Design}
\label{sec:research-design}

Design Science Research (DSR) was chosen as the research paradigm for this thesis because the project required both systematic problem investigation and artefact construction; neither activity alone would have been sufficient. The process model applied is the six-phase model proposed by \textcite{peffers2007dsr}, which structures artefact-building research from problem identification through to communication of results. The theoretical grounding for DSR in this thesis comes from \textcite{hevner2004dsr}, who establish DSR as a rigorous discipline distinct from behavioural science: where behavioural science seeks to explain natural phenomena, design science produces utility through construction. Both references are used in Chapter~2 to introduce DSR as a theoretical concept; this chapter describes how that methodology was applied in practice.

The six phases of the Peffers model were applied as follows in this project:

\begin{table}[htbp]
  \centering
  \caption{Peffers' six-phase DSR model as applied in this project.}
  \label{tab:dsr-phases}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}p{0.28\linewidth}p{0.65\linewidth}@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{DSR Phase} & \textbf{Application in this project} \\
    \midrule
    1.\ Problem identification
      & Semi-structured interviews with seven traffic coordinators across Norwegian transport companies. Identified manual assignment, tacit knowledge dependency, and legacy system limitations as core problems. \\
    \addlinespace
    2.\ Define objectives
      & Functional and non-functional requirements derived from interview findings. Fit/gap analysis comparing existing systems (Timpex, Trimtex, Opptur) against identified coordinator needs. Objective defined: a platform that generates optimised plans while keeping the coordinator in control. \\
    \addlinespace
    3.\ Design and development
      & Iterative development of Ressursplanlegger: planning interface, multi-engine optimisation (greedy solver, CP-SAT, Timefold), conflict detection, and driver/vehicle management. Agile sprints with requirements refined as domain understanding grew. \\
    \addlinespace
    4.\ Demonstration
      & The working system was demonstrated through a structured walkthrough using synthetic planning data, showing that the algorithm generates feasible plans and that the coordinator can review, manually override, and approve assignments through the planning interface. \\
    \addlinespace
    5.\ Evaluation
      & The system was assessed through two complementary methods: requirements traceability (mapping implemented features against requirements derived from the interviews) and algorithm benchmarking on synthetic datasets (measuring plan quality, constraint satisfaction, and solver performance across problem sizes). \\
    \addlinespace
    6.\ Communication
      & This thesis presents the problem, the designed artefact, and the findings to an academic audience. \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\textcite{wieringa2014dsr} separates \emph{validation} from \emph{evaluation}, and that distinction determines what this thesis can and cannot claim. Evaluation requires deploying the artefact with real stakeholders in a real operational context and measuring its effects on practice, while validation predicts the artefact's behaviour through controlled assessment without production deployment. This thesis performs validation. Ressursplanlegger was not operated by a real transport company during the thesis period; no real-world operational data was collected, and no coordinator used the system in their daily work. The claims this thesis makes about system utility are therefore predictions grounded in requirements traceability and algorithm benchmarks, not observations of practice. This is a genuine limitation. Moving from validation to evaluation would require a production pilot with at least one transport company over a sustained period — something outside the time and resource constraints of this thesis. The results in Chapter~5 should therefore be read as evidence that the system is likely to be useful, not as proof that it is.


\section{Data Collection}
\label{sec:data-collection}

Semi-structured interviews were used as the primary data collection method for this study. A semi-structured format gives the interviewer a set of prepared questions and themes to work through, while leaving space for the participant to raise concerns or details that were not anticipated when the guide was written \parencite{oates2022researching}. This combination of structure and openness was appropriate here because the domain was largely unexplored at the start of the project: the team knew broadly what to ask about (how coordinators assign drivers, what systems they use, what slows them down), but did not know in advance which pain points would prove most significant, or what variation would exist across companies. A fully structured questionnaire would have closed off unexpected findings; an entirely unstructured conversation would have made systematic comparison across seven interviews difficult. The semi-structured format balanced both concerns.

Seven participants were recruited through purposive sampling, selecting companies that would produce meaningful variation in the phenomena of interest: scale, current system maturity, and geographic context. The sample covers a range from Bergen Bulk Transport, which operates entirely without a planning system (8--20 drivers), through several Norlog branches that use Timpex (Norlog Lakselv, Norlog Mo i Rana) and Trimtex (Norlog Tana), to Harlem Solutions running Opptur, and Ottem, a larger operator with approximately 45 vehicles across three departments using a proprietary order system. Nordic Crane (contact: Svein) was included as a mixed crane-and-transport operator planning a system replacement. The sample is not random; it was assembled to cover the full spectrum from no system to custom system, across Norway from Lakselv in the far north to Bergen on the west coast. Companies were contacted directly on the basis of their known use of relevant systems or by referral.

The interview guide was organised around five themes: (1) current tools and daily workflow, (2) criteria used when assigning drivers to assignments, (3) how sick-leave substitution is handled, (4) attitudes toward automation and algorithm-generated plans, and (5) conditions for adoption of a new system. All questions were open-ended, inviting participants to describe their own practice rather than confirm or deny a predetermined list of options. The full interview guide is included as Appendix~A.

All seven interviews were conducted on 4 March 2026 as phone calls. Phone format was chosen for practical reasons: participants are distributed across Norway, and coordinators' working days are unpredictable. Calls were recorded with the participants' informed consent before recording began. The interviews lasted approximately 20--45 minutes each, reflecting differences in company complexity and how much participants chose to elaborate. All interviews were conducted in Norwegian, as this was the participants' working language and the most natural medium for discussing daily operational practice.

Interview recordings were transcribed using Sonix.ai, an automated transcription service, producing an initial draft transcript for each interview. The automated output was then reviewed and corrected manually to fix transcription errors, particularly for domain-specific terms (driver classifications, system names) and Norwegian dialect features that the automated service rendered inaccurately. Full corrected transcripts were retained in the project files and used as the primary input to the thematic analysis described in Section~\ref{sec:data-analysis}.


\section{Data Analysis}
\label{sec:data-analysis}

The seven interview transcripts were analysed using the thematic analysis framework described by \textcite{braun2006qualitative}, which proceeds through five phases: familiarisation, coding, theme generation, review, and definition. Thematic analysis was chosen because it is suited to exploratory interview data where the goal is inductive: generating categories from participant descriptions rather than testing a pre-specified framework. In the familiarisation phase, each corrected transcript was read in full and annotated with initial observations. Relevant passages were then coded inductively, attaching short descriptive labels to segments where a participant described a planning practice, a system limitation, or an attitude toward automation. The codes were subsequently grouped by similarity, and candidate themes were identified where multiple codes converged on a common underlying idea. The candidate themes were reviewed against the full transcripts to verify that they were grounded in the data and not artefacts of the initial coding framing. Five themes emerged from this process: (1) manual planning as the universal baseline, with no company using automatic driver assignment; (2) system slowness and legacy friction, particularly with Timpex and Trimtex under concurrent use; (3) sick-leave handling, ranging from a straightforward substitution at some companies to a daily operational burden at others; (4) attitudes toward automation, split between scepticism rooted in the unpredictability of transport operations and openness to algorithm-generated plans subject to human correction; and (5) key-person dependency and tacit knowledge, where critical operational knowledge about driver competencies, route familiarity, and customer relationships resided entirely in individual coordinators' heads and was not captured by any system.

The five themes were not the final product of the analysis; they served as the bridge between raw interview findings and structured system requirements. Each theme was examined for the concrete pain points it contained, and those pain points were translated into functional requirements using the MoSCoW prioritisation scheme (Must have, Should have, Could have, Won't have). MoSCoW was selected because it separates system-critical requirements from enhancements, a distinction that maps directly onto the iterative development process described in Section~\ref{sec:development-process}. For example, the tacit knowledge theme produced requirements for structured competence and certification records, work schedule registration, and constraint-aware assignment: capabilities the existing systems lacked. The sick-leave theme produced requirements for rapid manual reassignment and real-time availability filtering. A fit/gap analysis then compared the resulting requirements against the capabilities of the systems in use across the seven companies (Timpex, Trimtex, Opptur, and proprietary solutions), making explicit which needs were partially met, which were entirely unmet, and which were out of scope (see \Cref{sec:fitgap}). The findings in Chapter~4 are directly traceable to this sequence: from interview transcript to theme, from theme to requirement, from requirement to gap.

The analysis has real limitations that should be read alongside the findings. Seven interviews provide sufficient depth for identifying recurring patterns but cannot support claims about the Norwegian transport sector as a whole; the sample was assembled purposively for variation, not statistical representativeness. The companies that agreed to participate may have had stronger interest in better planning tools than those who declined, which introduces self-selection bias: the pain points identified may be more acute in this sample than in the sector broadly. All seven interviews were conducted on a single day, which removed any longitudinal perspective; the analysis captures how coordinators worked on and around 4 March 2026, not how their practices change across seasons or operational phases. These are expected trade-offs given the project scope; they limit how far the findings in Chapter~4 can be generalised and bear on how the discussion in Chapter~5 should be read.


\section{System Development Process}
\label{sec:development-process}

Ressursplanlegger was developed using an agile, iterative approach \parencite{beck2001agile}, with short cycles of planning, implementation, and review rather than a fixed upfront specification. Embret took primary responsibility for system development, while Mikael provided requirements and domain knowledge derived from the user research. The arrangement was intentionally lightweight: no formal Scrum ceremonies, no fixed sprint length, no separate product owner or scrum master role. The agile principles that mattered here were the practical ones: delivering working software in small increments and responding to new understanding as it emerged from the interviews and analysis. Process compliance was treated as secondary to both.

User research and development proceeded in parallel throughout the project. Interview findings fed directly into development priorities: themes identified during thematic analysis were translated into requirements, and those requirements shaped which features were implemented in the following development cycle. As the system took shape, it in turn clarified which requirements were genuinely difficult to implement and which design decisions required further input from domain knowledge. This mutual refinement is consistent with DSR's iterative design cycle, where each iteration between problem investigation and artefact construction deepens the understanding of both \parencite{peffers2007dsr}. The MoSCoW requirements described in Section~\ref{sec:data-analysis} acted as the stable reference point: requirements did not change arbitrarily, but the priority ordering and implementation details were revised as development revealed constraints that the upfront analysis had not anticipated.

The technology choices were driven by two criteria: fitness for the domain and support for the multi-engine benchmarking the research requires. The web application was built on Next.js with a PostgreSQL database accessed through Prisma, with tRPC providing type-safe communication between the frontend and server. The optimisation layer runs three solvers (a custom Python greedy baseline, Google OR-Tools CP-SAT, and Timefold Solver in Java), each invoked as a subprocess and selected at runtime. The multi-engine architecture was chosen specifically to allow solver comparison on the same problem instances, which is how algorithm performance is assessed in Chapter~4.5. The full technology justification is in Chapter~4.4.

The development progression followed the phases established in Table~\ref{tab:dsr-phases}, and the order of those phases was not incidental: each phase produced artefacts that constrained what the next phase could do. The project opened with problem investigation, culminating in the interviews conducted on 4 March 2026. Requirements derivation and fit/gap analysis followed immediately, producing the structured requirements described in Section~\ref{sec:data-analysis}; these requirements defined what the system had to do before a single line of implementation was written. Prototype development then ran in parallel with requirements refinement: a planning interface and basic data model came first, followed by driver and vehicle management, and then conflict detection. Algorithm implementation came last, not because it was least important but because it depended on the data model and planning interface being stable. The implementation progressed from the greedy solver through OR-Tools CP-SAT to Timefold, with a benchmarking framework added to compare solver outputs across synthetic problem instances. A final integration phase connected algorithm output to the planning interface and verified that constraint violations were surfaced correctly through the UI. The system was then assessed against the validation criteria described in Section~\ref{sec:research-design}.


\section{Validity and Reliability}
\label{sec:validity}

Malterud's four criteria for qualitative research quality provide the structure for assessing both the interview data and the system artefact in this study \parencite{malterud2003kvalitative}: systematic critical reflection, relevance, validity, and reflexivity. Systematic critical reflection means making the conditions under which knowledge was produced transparent enough that the reader can assess the claims independently. Relevance asks whether the findings can be used beyond the immediate study context. Validity concerns whether the study actually investigated what it set out to investigate, both in terms of internal coherence and consistency across the study. Reflexivity requires the researchers to account for how their own position and involvement may have shaped what was found and how it was interpreted. The following paragraphs address validity and reflexivity in detail; systematic critical reflection is satisfied through the transparent account of sampling choices, interview conditions, and data analysis steps provided in Sections~\ref{sec:data-collection}--\ref{sec:data-analysis}; relevance is addressed in the second paragraph of this section.

The interview data rests on a purposive sample of seven companies selected to represent meaningful variation in scale, geographic distribution, and system maturity. The sample ranges from a company of 8--20 drivers operating entirely without a planning system to a 45-vehicle operation running a proprietary order system, with geographic spread from Lakselv in the far north to Bergen on the west coast. Consistent findings across all seven interviews strengthen the internal validity of the themes: the core pain points (manual assignment, tacit knowledge dependency, slow legacy systems) emerged independently across companies rather than being driven by a single outlier. This cross-interview convergence serves as triangulation within the qualitative data. The limitation is external validity: seven companies, selected for variation rather than representativeness, cannot support generalisation to the Norwegian transport sector as a whole. The findings are credible within the sample; they should be treated as an informed basis for system requirements, not as sector-wide prevalence estimates. On the relevance criterion, the themes and requirements derived here are likely to apply to comparable SME transport operators with similar planning workflows, even if they cannot be claimed to hold sector-wide.

System validity in a DSR project concerns whether the artefact actually addresses the problem it was designed to solve. Two mechanisms serve this function here. First, a requirements traceability matrix maps each implemented feature to the functional requirements derived from the interviews, making it possible to check directly whether the identified needs were addressed in the system. Second, algorithm benchmarking on synthetic datasets assesses whether the optimisation engines produce feasible, constraint-satisfying plans across a range of problem sizes. As established in Section~\ref{sec:research-design}, this thesis performs validation rather than evaluation in the sense of \textcite{wieringa2014dsr}; the absence of production deployment is a genuine constraint on what can be claimed, and the validation provides grounds for prediction, not proof of impact.

The most significant limitation of this study is that the development team and the research team are the same people. This creates a structural risk of confirmation bias: design decisions made during development may have been assessed more favourably than they would have been by independent evaluators, because the researchers had a stake in finding that those decisions worked. This risk cannot be fully eliminated. It is mitigated through two structural measures: all requirements were derived from interview data before system design began, creating an external baseline that the system is assessed against rather than one constructed alongside it; and the requirements traceability matrix makes the mapping between needs and features explicit, allowing the reader to verify the claims independently. The bias risk is acknowledged here as an open limitation. A follow-up study using independent evaluators or production deployment data would provide stronger evidence.
