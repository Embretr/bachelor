## Sammendrag (2–3 setninger)

Venkatesh et al. (2003) synthesise eight competing user acceptance models into the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT), which posits four core determinants of technology adoption intention and use: performance expectancy, effort expectancy, social influence, and facilitating conditions. Four moderators — gender, age, experience, and voluntariness of use — condition the strength of each relationship. UTAUT was empirically validated across four organisations and cross-validated in two additional organisations, explaining up to 70 percent of variance in usage intention; its primary practical contribution is as a diagnostic framework for managers assessing the likelihood of successful technology introduction across different user populations and organisational contexts.

## Claims this source supports

### Claim 1: Performance expectancy is the strongest and most consistent predictor of behavioural intention to use a technology system

- **Suggested for:** Ch 5.1.3 ¶1 (adoption thresholds across the seven companies — companies will adopt when they believe Ressursplanlegger will help them attain job-related gains); Ch 5.2 ¶1 (cost/benefit threshold framing)
- **Direkte sitat:** "Performance expectancy is defined as the degree to which an individual believes that using the system will help him or her to attain gains in job performance. The performance expectancy construct within each individual model (Table 9) is the strongest predictor of intention and remains significant at all points of measurement in both voluntary and mandatory settings." (p. 447)
- **Parafrase:** Of the four UTAUT constructs, performance expectancy — whether users believe the system will improve their job outcomes — is the most robust determinant of adoption intention across all study contexts and time points.
- **Forbehold:** Effect moderated by gender and age: stronger for men and younger workers. In our context, the coordinator population is not characterised — gender/age moderators cannot be applied directly.
- **Anvendelse på vårt case:** For trafikkoordinatorer vurderer å ta i bruk Ressursplanlegger, er det primære adopsjonsspørsmålet om systemet faktisk reduserer overtid og ujevn last mellom sjåfører (dvs. Effektivitet-dimensjonene) — dette er performance expectancy operasjonalisert i transportdomenet. Selskaper med synlig ressursutnyttingsproblem har høyere performance expectancy og lavere adopsjonsterskel.

### Claim 2: Technology adoption decisions are highly contingent on organisational and contextual factors — a single universal adoption model is insufficient without moderator analysis

- **Suggested for:** Ch 5.1.3 ¶1 (cross-company adaptability); Ch 5.2 ¶1 (adoption barriers vary by company)
- **Direkte sitat:** "UTAUT underscores this point and highlights the importance of contextual analysis in developing strategies for technology implementation within organizations. While each of the existing models in the domain is quite successful in predicting technology usage behavior, it is only when one considers the complex range of potential moderating influences that a more complete picture of the dynamic nature of individual perceptions about technology begins to emerge." (p. 470)
- **Parafrase:** Predicting whether a system will be adopted requires contextual analysis of moderating factors (voluntariness, experience, gender, age, organisational setting) — no single model applies uniformly across organisations.
- **Forbehold:** The paper's moderators are individual-level (gender, age, experience) and situational (voluntariness). Structural organisational differences (fleet size, operational rules) are not captured — these are specific to our multi-company Tilpasningsdyktighet problem.
- **Anvendelse på vårt case:** Adopsjonsterskelen for Ressursplanlegger varierer på tvers av de syv intervjuede selskapene avhengig av organisatorisk kontekst (flåtestørrelse, eksisterende systemmodning, operasjonelle regler) — dette er den domene-spesifikke instansen av UTAUT-moderatoranalysen. Ulike soft-constraint-vekter per selskap er den tekniske mekanismen som operasjonaliserer kontekstsensitivitet.

### Claim 3: Social influence — whether important others in the organisation support use — is significant primarily under mandatory implementation conditions, not voluntary ones

- **Suggested for:** Ch 5.1.3 ¶1 (adoption dynamics in Norwegian transport companies where coordinator use may be quasi-mandatory); Ch 5.2 ¶1
- **Direkte sitat:** "Social influence is defined as the degree to which an individual perceives that important others believe he or she should use the new system. [...] None of the social influence constructs are significant in voluntary contexts; however, each becomes significant when use is mandated." (p. 451)
- **Parafrase:** Peer/supervisor pressure to use a system matters for adoption only when use is not purely voluntary — in mandatory or quasi-mandatory deployment contexts it becomes a significant driver.
- **Forbehold:** In our context, coordinators were not mandated to use Ressursplanlegger during this thesis — it was validated but not deployed. The claim applies prospectively to a real deployment scenario.
- **Anvendelse på vårt case:** Dersom et selskap innfører Ressursplanlegger som et arbeidskrav (ikke frivillig), vil koordinatorens sosiale kontekst (lederstøtte, kollegabruk) bli en signifikant adopsjonsdriver — relevant for §5.2 anbefalinger om implementeringsstrategi.

### Claim 4: Effort expectancy — perceived ease of use — matters most in early stages of use and declines in importance as users gain experience

- **Suggested for:** Ch 5.2 ¶1 (deployment-readiness discussion); Ch 5.1.3 ¶1
- **Direkte sitat:** "Effort expectancy is defined as the degree of ease associated with the use of the system. - **Parafrase:** Ease of use is a relevant adoption concern at introduction, but recedes as users become familiar with the system; it does not sustain as a long-term adoption driver.

## Forbehold og begrensninger

- **Domain mismatch (most important):** UTAUT was developed and validated in generic IT workplace settings (accounting software, meeting managers, banking portfolio tools, retail service tools). It does not address domain-specific features of transport resource planning — operational urgency, safety constraints, or the operator-vs-owner asymmetry. The writer must explicitly bridge the construct to our domain; direct quotation of UTAUT findings as claims about transport coordinators is not permitted.

- **Individual vs. organisational unit of analysis:** UTAUT's four constructs and four moderators operate at the individual level (one person's acceptance decision). Our thesis's Tilpasningsdyktighet concern is at the organisational level (will *a company* adopt?). These are different questions. UTAUT supports the claim that adoption varies across organisational contexts; it does not directly address cross-company technical adaptability.

- **No cost/benefit threshold construct:** The outline's §5.1.3 ¶1 marker cites UTAUT for "cost / benefit thresholds across the seven interviewed companies." UTAUT does not define a cost/benefit threshold construct. The closest construct is performance expectancy (perceived utility) combined with effort expectancy (perceived cost of learning/using). The writer must operationalise "cost/benefit threshold" through these two constructs — not cite UTAUT as directly defining such a threshold.

- **No "willingness to pay" construct:** UTAUT says nothing about pricing or financial willingness to pay. If the thesis discusses financial adoption barriers, that requires a different source.

- **Voluntariness: definition mismatch with our case:** UTAUT's voluntariness moderator is a perceived individual-level variable (does the person feel free to not use the system?). In our thesis, coordinator use of Ressursplanlegger is voluntary in the thesis context and would likely be quasi-mandatory in a real deployment. This distinction matters when citing the social influence findings — cite the mandatory-context finding, not the voluntary-context finding.

- **No MUST CITE marker for §2.2 HITL:** UTAUT is not a HITL theory. Do not cite it in §2.2 or anywhere that requires a trust/control theoretical foundation. UTAUT supports adoption-likelihood claims only.

- **MUST CITE marker fit check:** outline §5.1.3 ¶1 has `MUST CITE: Venkatesh — UTAUT (technology adoption thresholds across organisational contexts)` and §5.2 ¶1 has `MUST CITE: Venkatesh — UTAUT (cost-benefit threshold framing for adoption)`. Claim 1 (performance expectancy as adoption driver) and Claim 2 (contextual analysis) both support these markers. However, the writer must operationalise "threshold" as performance expectancy rather than citing a threshold construct that UTAUT does not define.

## Key arguments / lines of reasoning

### Argument: UTAUT as diagnostic tool for managers

- **Premiss 1:** Existing adoption models (TAM, TPB, etc.) each explain 17–53% of variance in isolation and have been tested primarily in voluntary, academic, or simple-technology settings.
- **Premiss 2:** The moderating influences of gender, age, experience, and voluntariness are critical but handled inconsistently across existing models.
- **Premiss 3:** A unified model integrating these moderators explains 70% of variance and is validated in real organisations with complex, mandatory technology introductions.
- **Konklusjon:** UTAUT "provides a useful tool for managers needing to assess the likelihood of success for new technology introductions and helps them understand the drivers of acceptance in order to proactively design interventions." (p. 425–426)
- **Sted:** Abstract, pp. 425–426; Discussion, p. 467
- **Hvilke claims dette støtter:** Ch 5.1.3 ¶1, Ch 5.2 ¶1

### Argument: Contextual analysis is required — no one-size-fits-all adoption strategy

- **Premiss:** The strength of each UTAUT predictor varies across contexts (voluntary vs. mandatory, early vs. late experience, different demographic groups).
- **Konklusjon:** "it is only when one considers the complex range of potential moderating influences that a more complete picture of the dynamic nature of individual perceptions about technology begins to emerge." (p. 470)
- **Sted:** p. 470
- **Hvilke claims dette støtter:** Ch 5.1.3 ¶1 (different companies require different adoption approaches); Ch 5.2 ¶1
