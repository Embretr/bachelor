## Sammendrag (2–3 setninger)

Orlikowski and Baroudi (1991) demonstrate empirically that 96.8% of IS research published between 1983 and 1988 rests on a single positivist philosophical stance, and argue that this dominance is unnecessarily restrictive given the social complexity of information technology phenomena. They present two alternative research philosophies — interpretive and critical — each characterised by distinctive ontological, epistemological, and methodological assumptions, and conclude that a plurality of perspectives enriches IS inquiry. The paper's primary contribution to this thesis is its taxonomic classification of IS research paradigms, which provides the vocabulary and justification for explaining why DSR is positioned alongside, rather than as a replacement for, positivist and interpretivist alternatives.

## Forbehold og begrensninger

- The paper does not mention Design Science Research or artefact-producing paradigms. It cannot be cited as endorsing DSR; it can only be cited as providing the vocabulary for naming what DSR is NOT (not positivist, not purely interpretivist).
- The three-philosophy taxonomy draws heavily on Chua (1986). In the thesis, citing Orlikowski & Baroudi (1991) is appropriate because it is the IS-specific adaptation of Chua's framework and is the standard IS methods citation for this purpose.
- The survey is restricted to 1983–1988 US journals. The empirical claim about 96.8% positivism is a historical observation; it should be introduced as such if used.
- The paper does not address transport planning, scheduling, resource optimisation, coordinators, or any domain-specific topic. It is a pure IS methodology paper.
- The paper advocates for pluralism among positivist / interpretive / critical philosophies — it does not discuss the DSR paradigm (which did not exist as a named category in 1991). Any inference that Orlikowski & Baroudi endorse DSR is anachronistic.

## Rammeverk og modeller

### Three-Philosophy Taxonomy with Figure 6 Belief Structure (pp. 7–8)

| Dimension of belief | Positivist | Interpretive | Critical |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ontology | Objective social world, independent of humans; discoverable by researchers | Social reality socially constructed through human action and interaction | Reality shaped by historical, structural, and ideological conditions |
| Epistemology | Empirical testability; hypothetic-deductive logic; universal laws | Understanding of meaning systems; circular/reciprocal causality; no universal laws | Critique of taken-for-granted assumptions; dialectical analysis |
| Methodology | Surveys, controlled experiments, structured instrumentation, inferential statistics | Field studies, ethnography, in-depth interviews, participant-derived categories | Critical discourse analysis, historical analysis, emancipatory intent |
| Theory–practice relationship | Technical: knowledge enables prediction and control | Interpretive: researcher always implicated; no value-neutral stance | Critical: knowledge should transform oppressive conditions |

The framework is the primary contribution of the paper. Figure 6 (p. 8) summarises the belief dimensions; Table/Figure 5 (p. 6) reports the empirical distribution.

## Key arguments / lines of reasoning

### Argument: The dominance of positivism limits IS inquiry
- **Premiss(er):** 96.8% of IS research in 1983–1988 rests on positivist assumptions (empirical finding). Positivism, when applied to social phenomena, disregards historical context, assumes determinate causal relations, and cannot discover non-deterministic or reciprocal relationships.
- **Konklusjon:** "The dominance of positivism, by not acknowledging the legitimacy of other research traditions, has limited what aspects of information systems phenomena we have studied, and how we have studied them." (p. 7)
- **Sted:** pp. 7–8
- **Hvilke claims dette støtter:** Ch 2.4 ¶2; Ch 3.2 ¶1

### Argument: Researchers should make deliberate, reflective paradigm choices
- **Premiss(er):** Research methods and assumptions are shaped by institutional context (doctoral programmes, journal norms, funding); they are not learned in a vacuum. Choosing a research approach determines which aspects of a phenomenon are revealed.
- **Konklusjon:** "researchers should ensure that they adopt a perspective that is compatible with their own research interests and predispositions, while remaining open to the possibility of other assumptions and interests." (p. 24)
