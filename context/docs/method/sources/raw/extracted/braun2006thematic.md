## Sammendrag (2–3 setninger)

Braun and Clarke (2006) argue that thematic analysis is an accessible and theoretically flexible qualitative analytic method that can be applied across a wide range of epistemological positions, and they provide a canonical 6-phase step-by-step guide for conducting it rigorously. The paper distinguishes inductive (data-driven) from theoretical (deductive/top-down) approaches, semantic from latent theme identification, and essentialist/realist from constructionist framings, and ends with a 15-point quality checklist. It positions thematic analysis as a foundational skill for qualitative researchers, suitable for identifying patterns of meaning across interview data sets of the kind used in this thesis.

## Claims this source supports

### Claim: "Thematic analysis is a method for identifying, analysing and reporting patterns (themes) within data."

- **Suggested for:** Ch 3.4 ¶1 (primary citation for the TA method chosen); also citable in Ch 3.3 ¶1 as background for why TA suits semi-structured interview data
- **Direkte sitat:** "Thematic analysis is a method for identifying, analysing and reporting patterns (themes) within data. It minimally organizes and describes your data set in (rich) detail." (p. 79)
- **Parafrase:** TA identifies and reports recurring patterns of meaning across a data set, ranging from minimal description to interpretive analysis.
- **Forbehold:** Definition is deliberately broad; the paper warns that TA's flexibility is both a strength and a source of inconsistency if not explicitly positioned.
- **Anvendelse på vårt case:** In §3.4, this definition anchors the claim that thematic analysis was selected as the analytic method for the seven semi-structured interviews with trafikkoordinatorer, framing the five themes (current tools, assignment criteria, sick-leave, automation attitudes, adoption criteria) as pattern-level findings rather than mere interview-guide summaries.

### Claim: "Thematic analysis is not wedded to any pre-existing theoretical framework, and therefore it can be used within different theoretical frameworks"

- **Suggested for:** Ch 3.4 ¶1 (justification for choosing TA within a DSR framework); Ch 3.2 ¶1 (supports the DSR compatibility argument)
- **Direkte sitat:** "thematic analysis is not wedded to any pre-existing theoretical framework, and therefore it can be used within different theoretical frameworks (although not all), and can be used to do different things within them." (p. 81)
- **Parafrase:** Unlike IPA or grounded theory, TA does not require a specific epistemological commitment, making it compatible with DSR's pragmatic, artefact-oriented research paradigm.
- **Forbehold:** The paper immediately qualifies "although not all" — the researcher must still state their epistemological position explicitly. A realist framing (appropriate for DSR problem-identification work) is one of three options the paper identifies.
- **Anvendelse på vårt case:** This claim justifies using TA within a DSR project — the interviews serve the DSRM "Problem Identification and Motivation" activity, not a standalone qualitative study. The theoretical flexibility of TA allows it to function as a problem-scoping instrument rather than a theory-generating one.

### Claim: TA offers an "accessible and theoretically flexible approach to analysing qualitative data"

- **Suggested for:** Ch 3.4 ¶1 (methodological positioning sentence)
- **Direkte sitat:** "we argue that it offers an accessible and theoretically flexible approach to analysing qualitative data." (p. 77, abstract)

### Claim: TA phases are recursive, not linear — the process moves back and forth between phases as needed

- **Suggested for:** Ch 3.4 ¶1 (process description); Ch 5.6 Methodology Reflection
- **Direkte sitat:** "analysis is not a linear process of simply moving from one phase to the next. Instead, it is more recursive process, where movement is back and forth as needed, throughout the phases." (p. 86)
- **Parafrase:** The six phases describe a logical order, not a rigid sequence — re-coding and theme revision continue throughout the analysis.
- **Forbehold:** The recursive nature is a feature of good TA, not a licence for unstructured coding. The paper's Table 2 (15-point checklist) establishes the quality floor.
- **Anvendelse på vårt case:** In §3.4, this supports reporting that coding and theme generation were iterative across the seven interview transcripts — themes such as the resource-utilization visibility gap were refined across multiple coding passes, not identified in a single sweep.

### Claim: An inductive approach codes data "without trying to fit it into a pre-existing coding frame, or the researcher's analytic preconceptions"

- **Suggested for:** Ch 3.4 ¶1 (explicitly characterises the approach used as inductive); Ch 3.4 ¶3 (methodological constraints — the approach was data-driven)
- **Direkte sitat:** "Inductive analysis is therefore a process of coding the data without trying to fit it into a pre-existing coding frame, or the researcher's analytic preconceptions. In this sense, this form of thematic analysis is data-driven." (p. 83)
- **Parafrase:** In inductive TA, themes emerge from the data itself rather than from theory or prior hypotheses — relevant because the thesis did not begin with a predefined list of coordinator pain points.
- **Forbehold:** The paper notes that complete epistemological neutrality is impossible (p. 84); the researcher cannot fully escape theoretical commitments. This is particularly relevant given author affiliation with Admmit (thesis limitation L3).
- **Anvendelse på vårt case:** The interviews were conducted without pre-coding the themes — the visibility gap and the operator-vs-owner asymmetry were not hypothesised in advance. §3.4 should characterise the approach as primarily inductive, with the caveat that a DSR frame (Peffers' Problem Identification activity) provided a broad orienting question.

### Claim: "The 'keyness' of a theme is not necessarily dependent on quantifiable measures — but rather on whether it captures something important in relation to the overall research question."

- **Suggested for:** Ch 3.4 ¶1 (justifies why themes with limited frequency — e.g., operator-vs-owner asymmetry — are treated as significant); Ch 4.1 ¶2 (supports naming visibility gap as the "primary surprising theme" despite not appearing in all seven interviews identically)
- **Direkte sitat:** "the 'keyness' of a theme is not necessarily dependent on quantifiable measures – but rather on whether it captures something important in relation to the overall research question." (p. 82)
- **Parafrase:** Theme significance is determined by theoretical/analytic relevance, not by counting how many participants mentioned a topic.
- **Forbehold:** This does not mean prevalence is irrelevant — the paper discusses prevalence extensively. It means qualitative criteria override quantitative thresholds.
- **Anvendelse på vårt case:** The operator-vs-owner asymmetry finding may not appear verbatim across all seven interviews, but its keyness for the thesis RQ (accountability to the coordinator) is analytically high. §4.1 and §5.1.1 can cite Braun & Clarke here to justify the asymmetry's status as a central finding despite variable interview frequency.

## Forbehold og begrensninger

- **Domain gap:** All of Braun & Clarke's own examples come from psychology / gender studies research. The method transfers directly, but the domain bridge (applying TA to organisational/logistics interviews in Norwegian transport) must be articulated by the thesis writer, not assumed from the source.
- **Psychology framing:** The paper is explicitly titled "in psychology" — the thesis should acknowledge this in §3.4 either by citing the broad applicability claim (p. 81: "in and beyond psychology") or by noting that TA is widely used in organisational and IS research.
- **Single-researcher vs team:** Braun & Clarke's examples often involve one researcher. This thesis had two researchers on the dev/research team — the reflexivity implications (split analysis roles, checking for inter-analyst consistency) are not addressed in this source.
- **No sample-size formula:** The paper does not provide a minimum or recommended interview count. The 7-interview justification must rest on purposive sampling logic (§3.3 ¶2) and the DSR Problem Identification scope, not solely on Braun & Clarke.
- **Coding software:** The paper acknowledges software-assisted coding (p. 89) but does not advocate for it — the thesis used manual/light tooling; this is consistent with TA practice.
- **Quality checklist (Table 2) as normative standard:** The 15 criteria are aspirational, not verified for this thesis. §3.7 should not claim all 15 are met; it should describe the process and acknowledge where time constraints limited phase completeness (e.g., thoroughness of re-coding in Phase 4).

## Rammeverk og modeller

### Six-Phase Thematic Analysis Framework (Table 1, p. 87)

| Phase | Name | Description (verbatim from Table 1) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Familiarizing yourself with your data | Transcribing data (if necessary), reading and re-reading the data, noting down initial ideas. |
| 2 | Generating initial codes | Coding interesting features of the data in a systematic fashion across the entire data set, collating data relevant to each code. |
| 3 | Searching for themes | Collating codes into potential themes, gathering all data relevant to each potential theme. |
| 4 | Reviewing themes | Checking if the themes work in relation to the coded extracts (Level 1) and the entire data set (Level 2), generating a thematic 'map' of the analysis. |
| 5 | Defining and naming themes | Ongoing analysis to refine the specifics of each theme, and the overall story the analysis tells, generating clear definitions and names for each theme. |
| 6 | Producing the report | The final opportunity for analysis. Selection of vivid, compelling extract examples, final analysis of selected extracts, relating back of the analysis to the research question and literature, producing a scholarly report of the analysis. |

**Application to this thesis:** Phase 1 = familiarisation with 7 transcripts (automated + manual correction). Phase 2 = coding coordinator statements by assignment criteria, pain points, automation attitudes, workflow. Phase 3 = grouping codes into the five thematic areas reported in §4.1. Phase 4 = cross-interview review for coherence and completeness. Phase 5 = naming and defining themes as reported in §4.1 (e.g., "resource-utilization visibility gap"). Phase 6 = writing §4.1 as the thematic report.

## Key arguments / lines of reasoning

### Argument: TA's flexibility requires explicit epistemological positioning

- **Premiss(er):** TA can be essentialist/realist, constructionist, or contextualist. Any theoretical framework carries assumptions about the nature of data and reality. A good TA makes these transparent.
- **Konklusjon:** Researchers must explicitly state whether their TA is inductive or theoretical, semantic or latent, and what epistemological position it occupies.
- **Sted:** pp. 81–82, 85
- **Hvilke claims dette støtter:** Ch 3.4 ¶1 — the thesis should state that the TA was primarily inductive, semantic-level, and essentialist/realist (coordinator experiences reported as real phenomena for DSR problem identification).

### Argument: Theme significance is analytically determined, not statistically

- **Premiss(er):** Prevalence is not the primary criterion for including a theme. What matters is whether a theme captures something important in relation to the research question.
- **Konklusjon:** A theme appearing in two out of seven interviews can be as significant as one appearing in all seven, if analytically important.
- **Sted:** p. 82
- **Hvilke claims dette støtter:** Ch 4.1 ¶2 and §5.1.1 — the visibility gap / operator-vs-owner asymmetry qualifies as a primary finding by analytic significance, not frequency count.

### Argument: The researcher actively constructs themes — they do not "emerge"

- **Premiss(er):** The language of "themes emerging" is a passive account that denies the researcher's constitutive role in selecting, identifying, and naming patterns.
- **Konklusjon:** Researchers should write actively: "the analysis identified X" not "X emerged from the data".
- **Sted:** pp. 80, 97 (Table 2 criterion 15)
- **Hvilke claims dette støtter:** §3.4 and §4.1 writing style — the thesis should use active analytic language when describing the five themes.

## Forfatter-perspektiv / metodologi

Braun and Clarke write from a critical/feminist psychology tradition (University of Auckland; University of the West of England). Their own examples draw on gender/sexuality research. The paper's normative stance is that TA should be a "named and claimed" method — researchers must consciously own their analytic choices rather than unreflectively applying a vague "qualitative analysis" label. This epistemological self-awareness norm is directly applicable to the DSR context: the thesis should claim TA explicitly for the interview analysis phase rather than describing it generically.
