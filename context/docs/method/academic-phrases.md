# Academic Phrases and Expressions

A collection of useful phrases for academic writing, organized by function.

---

## Referring to Previous Studies Without Taking a Position

These phrases can be used when presenting others' ideas or research in a neutral manner:

- A common/widespread/prevailing view is …
- According to recent/some/a number of studies … [reference to the studies]
- Some/many (researchers/scholars) have been concerned with / emphasize that … [reference to the studies]
- A (relatively) common viewpoint is …
- The discussion concerns …
- The main findings here are …

---

## Referring to Previous Studies and Building on Them

These phrases can be used to introduce ideas you agree with and build upon:

- As Hansen (2020) has demonstrated/shown/documented/points out/highlights …
- In agreement with Hansen (2020), it can be argued that …
- I will use Gundersen's definition as a basis when discussing X
- Here I draw on / take as my starting point …
- Gundersen's distinction between X and Y can be used to explain …
- An important starting point for this study is [theory X], which …

---

## Critiquing Previous Studies and Theories

These phrases can be used to point out weaknesses or limitations of a theory, explanation, etc., thereby supporting the relevance of your own project:

- Most studies of X have (only) focused on …
- A limitation/potential weakness of such an approach is that …
- This approach may be inadequate insofar as/if/in cases where …
- Previous research/studies have been limited to / have not taken into account …
- [Research X] is, however, not very specific regarding …
- *Your field* is a relatively new field of research, and therefore has no/little …
- Previous studies have tended to focus on X rather than Y
- Previous studies are incomplete with regard to …
- The existing research does not explain how/why …
- It has not been possible to replicate/generalize these results, because …
- The results are based on data from (e.g. 1990), and it is uncertain whether the same would hold true today
- This is a controversial topic, and researchers have not reached consensus on …
- Gundersen does not acknowledge / overlooks / does not account for / does not consider …
- A weakness of the study is/appears to be …
- The study does not explain how X differs from Y …
- A (significant/major) weakness/problem/shortcoming of the theory/method/explanation/argument is …
- The theory does not explain how / does not account for …
- A possible criticism of [the theory] is that …
- A question one must/can ask is …
- Gundersen's argument relies too heavily on …
- A limitation of this explanation is …

### Constructive Suggestions for Improvement

These phrases can be used to offer constructive suggestions, thereby supporting your own perspective:

- The study could have been more interesting/relevant if …
- If the study had focused on … one could …
- The study could beneficially …
- The question should rather be …

---

## Defining Concepts

These phrases can be used when defining a term:

- *The term* is (often/usually/commonly) defined as …
- A common definition of *the term* is …
- Many have defined *the term* as …
- The definition I employ here is …
- Gundersen (2002) defines *the term* as …
- By *the term* I mean here …

---

## Formulating Research Questions

These phrases can be used to formulate one or more questions that your text aims to answer:

- Based on X, this paper will address the following question: "How/In what way …?"
- To investigate [this], the central question will be / it will be essential to learn how …
- The question to be answered/explored in the following is whether there exists/is/could be …
- I will therefore address the following question: …
- The research question of this paper is as follows: …
- The main question is …
- To address the research question, I will examine three sub-questions: A, B, and C.

---

## Describing Methods and Methodological Choices

### Describing Previously Used Methods

- There are several methods for measuring X, such as *method*, *method*, and *method*
- Many have used *the method* to measure/examine/study …
- A widely used method for investigating … is *the method*
- *The method* is the most common/widely used method for investigating …

### Justifying Your Choice of Method

- A suitable way to investigate this is *the method*
- In order to investigate X, it will be appropriate to use *the method*
- Advantages of *the method* are …
- Due to X and Y, *the method* may be a suitable approach

### Acknowledging Methodological Weaknesses

- A prerequisite for using *the method* is …
- Limitations/weaknesses/problems/disadvantages of using *the method* may be …
- *The method* does not yield reliable results with regard to …

## Making Comparisons

### Introducing Differences

- X differs from Y in several ways …
- X is closely related to Y, except …
- X has many similarities with Y, but differs in …
- Gundersen has found/pointed out/identified important differences between X and Y …
- In contrast to previous studies, we found no evidence that X is the case
- Gundersen found significant differences in the occurrence of X in …
- Where X concerns Xi, Y concerns Yi
- This interpretation differs from / stands in contrast to Gundersen and Hansen's interpretation. They argue that …

### Introducing Similarities

- There are a number of similarities between X and Y. First … Second …
- X and Y share several characteristics, such as …
- The effects of X resemble the effects of Y, namely …
- This definition resembles Gundersen (2022), which holds that …
- These results can be compared with …
- The approach in this study resembles other studies of X
- Studies have compared X among Norwegian and American students, and found key similarities

### Indicating Differences Across Two Sentences

> In democratic societies, citizens have political influence.

- This stands in contrast to …
- This is in opposition to …
- This is how democracy differs from …

> … the form of government in a dictatorship, where political influence is limited.

### Indicating Similarities Across Two Sentences

> Citizens in Norway elect representatives to the Storting (Parliament).

- Similarly
- Likewise
- In a similar manner

> … Americans elect representatives to the Senate and Congress.

## Giving Examples

### Introducing Examples

- For example, Hansen and Gundersen conducted an experiment in which they …
- Another example of what is meant by X is …
- An example of this is Gundersen's study of …
- This is demonstrated in the study …
- X illustrates well what is meant by Y, namely …
- This can be illustrated by looking at …
- A similar case in England shows that …

### Describing the Type of Example

A …

- well-known
- widely used
- central
- good
- classic
- useful
- important
- illustrative

… example of X is …

### Using Examples to Support an Argument

- This (example) shows/illustrates that …
- As this example shows, it is important to …
- This example demonstrates the need for …
- This example shows how …
- The example presented here supports the assumption/idea/claim that …

## Argumentation and Discussion

### Making Your Own Argumentation Visible

- My main claim is that …
- The main claim of this paper is …
- The claim/idea I will argue for is …
- The point I wish to highlight is …
- This paper is based on three central claims. These are …
- I will examine/support the claim by …
- I will argue for X by referring to …
- I will discuss some common objections to this argument …
- This can be claimed/asserted with certainty because …
- I have now addressed three arguments for X. In the following section, I will focus on objections to these arguments.
- As I argue/have argued …

### Questioning an Idea or Claim

- The discussion has addressed X and Y. A question one might ask is …
- Although … it can nevertheless be argued/maintained that …
- The problem with this account of X is …
- A possible criticism of X is …
- Although there may be good reasons to claim X, one can ask whether …
- It has nevertheless not been established that …
- In light of X, one might ask whether …
- Is it reasonable to assume that … or could it also …?
- Another possible explanation could be …
- In contrast to X, it appears that …

### Introducing Alternative or New Perspectives

- An equally interesting angle could be …
- One might also ask whether X might just as well …
- From such a perspective, it (also) becomes clear that …
- To study/understand/illuminate X, it would be interesting to look at …
- In light of X, it may appear that …
- If X is the case, it could also …
- A plausible explanation is …
- An alternative interpretation …

---

## Concluding a Line of Argumentation

These phrases can be used to conclude a paragraph:

- This shows that …
- The result of what has been demonstrated/presented so far is that …
- On the basis of X, it can be argued that …
- Therefore/thus there are good/sufficient reasons to claim …
- Finally / overall, it can be said that …
- A concluding point is that …
- The purpose of this is to show …

---

## Creating Flow in the Text

These phrases can be used to guide the reader through the text and make the argumentation easier to follow:

- As (previously) mentioned …
- As I have previously emphasized …
- Based on [the question], I will look at …
- To illuminate [the question], it will be necessary to look at / address …
- In the next paragraph, I describe / I will address …
- In the following / Furthermore, I will argue for …
- … after the discussion of X, I will address Y
- I now turn my attention to …
- In light of X, I will now discuss Y.
- Before we continue with X, it is important to emphasize that Y.
- So far I have focused on X. Next, I will …
- Before I explain theory A, I will …
- With a definition of X, we can now look at …
- As I have shown, X, Y, and Z. I/the paper now turns to a discussion of …
- On the basis of X, we have seen that …
- The chapter has addressed … The next chapter will focus on discussing …
- In the following, I will elaborate on this discussion by …

## Discussing Findings and Results

### Results Consistent with Previous Studies

- The study confirms that X is associated with …
- This result is supported by …
- The finding is consistent with Gundersen's 2003 study, which …
- Compared with previous studies, the results confirm that …
- This is also consistent with earlier observations showing that …
- There are similarities between these findings/observations/results and what emerges in …
- The observations are supported by …

### Results That Contradict Previous Studies

- In contrast to previous studies, our results show that …
- These findings show the opposite of previous studies, namely …
- Where previous studies have shown X, these findings/results point toward …
- Hansen showed in 2003 that …
- This differs from this study in …
- It has previously been claimed that …
- This turns out not to be the case, because …
- Compared with previous research …
- Another view of …

---

## Nuancing, Making Reservations, Indicating Uncertainty

These phrases can be used to convey that there may be multiple causes for something:

- B may be a result of A.
- B is likely a consequence of A.
- It is probable that A leads to B.
- There is reason to believe that B is caused by A.
- A probable/possible explanation is/may be …
- An explanation that cannot be ruled out is …
- The occurrence of A can be attributed to B
- There are several possible explanations/causes …
- The reason the results diverge may be …
- A possible explanation for the variation in the results is …
- A possible/reasonable interpretation may be …

---

## Conclusion

### Closing with a Summary

- The analysis/study of … has provided insight into …
- The main arguments in the analysis have been that …
- One of the central results of this study is that …

### Concluding

- The aim/purpose of the analysis/study is, as described in the introduction, to answer the question / *the research question*
- The research question / the starting point for this study/analysis was …
- The answer to [the question] is that …
- As the study shows, there are good reasons to claim that …
- The conclusion of the study is that …
- The study shows how …
- The data/findings from this study show that …
- To conclude, I/we will highlight …
- The most important conclusion / the main conclusion is …

### Demonstrating the Significance of Your Study

- The study/analysis thereby contributes …
- The study provides insight into factors that are important regarding …
- With regard to improvement/prevention/understanding of X, this study may serve as a contribution.
